Limitations
Limitations are meaningful restrictions attached to specific Powers or Power Sets. Each Limitation grants
1 additional Power Pick in exchange for a real constraint on how a power functions. They are not general
life complications — those belong in Merits and Flaws. A Limitation must directly affect the use of a
specific Power or Power Set. The number of Limitations a hero can take is capped by Campaign Rank, as
shown in the table above.

Power Picks and Advancement
Power growth is based on total character level, not individual class level. A multiclassed hero gains Power
Picks on the same schedule as any other hero. Power Picks may be spent on any Power Set the hero
qualifies for, regardless of which class those levels belong to.
All other advancement — Talents, ASI Picks, Skill Picks — follows the standard Advancement Table
based on total character level.
Beyond Level 10
Level 10 is the ceiling. It does not have to be the end.
For campaigns that continue past Level 10 — heroes who have reached their peak and keep showing up
anyway — HEROIC 5e provides a framework for continued growth that respects the ceiling while
rewarding the players who earned it.
Legacy Advances
After reaching Level 10, a hero earns one Legacy Advance every ten sessions of play. A Legacy Advance
represents the kind of growth that comes not from raw power climbing but from experience, reflection, and
time — the things that happen to a hero after they have become who they are.
At each Legacy Advance, choose one of the following:
Legacy Advance Options
● Deepen. Gain one Power Pick. This pick may be spent on Refinements or Branch Powers within
Power Sets the hero already possesses. It may not be used to purchase a new Power Set or a
new Core Track entry.

This chapter covers everything about the Powers system: structure, Power Picks, Power types, the attack

Sets themselves are in Chapter 11. Read this framework first — once the language is clear, every Power

Design Principles
The Powers system follows these principles without exception.
● Powers are superhero-native. Built for comic-book action, not fantasy spellcasting repainted in
capes. A fire controller does not prepare fire spells and manage spell slots. They control fire. A
telepath is not expending mental energy on every surface read. They are psychic. Powers
express who the hero is.
● Heroes feel complete at Level 1. Campaign Rank defines power scale. A World Class hero
beginning play at Level 1 already feels World Class. Level measures experience and refinement.
Campaign Rank measures the scale of fiction the heroes belong to.
● No Power Points. HEROIC 5e does not use resource pools to fuel Powers. At-Will Powers are
always available. Encounter Powers refresh each encounter. Daily Powers are available once per
day safely. The Edge economy handles dramatic pushes beyond that baseline.
● Clear mechanics. Powers use attack rolls, saves, action types, Advantage, Disadvantage,
Prowess, durations, and conditions. Every power entry states exactly what it does. No power
relies on vague language that requires interpretation at the table.
● Concept chooses expression. The same Power Set can look completely different depending on

Power Sets and Core Tracks
A Power Set is the family of related abilities a hero can access: Energy Generation, Super Strength,
Telepathy, Shadow Control, and so on. Every significant Power Set has a Core Track — the spine of the
Power Set that defines the hero's scale, access, and identity within that set.
Core Track steps are purchased in order. You cannot take Core Track 2 without Core Track 1.

Power Stunt: Emulation
Emulation is a specific subset of the Power Stunt rules — its most demanding expression, not a separate
system. Emulation allows a hero to reach beyond what they have purchased and produce the full effect of
a specific unpurchased Branch Power within an owned Power Set. Where a standard Power Stunt
produces an improvised reduced-scale effect, Emulation forces the hero's powers to do something their
body is not yet built for — at full effect, at full cost.
Emulation is not improvisation. It is not a shortcut. It exists for the moment when the situation demands
something the hero cannot yet do and the player is willing to pay the real price.

Reading Power Set Entries
Each Power Set entry in Chapter 11 follows a consistent format. Every entry opens with a description of
what the Power Set represents in superhero fiction and what kinds of heroes use it. The following
sections appear in every entry:
● Suggested Attributes. Which Attributes most commonly govern this Power Set and what each
choice represents. Some Power Sets have a fixed Governing Ability. Most offer two or three
options.
● Associated Conditions. The conditions this Power Set most commonly inflicts, exploits, or
resists — indicating its tactical role in combat.
● Default Damage Track. For Power Sets with a repeated attack pattern, the baseline formula for
unarmed strikes or attacks from this Power Set.
● Core Track. The four Core Track steps in order, each a passive benefit defining scale and
unlocking higher-Tier power access.
● Combat Powers. All At-Will, Encounter, Daily, and Reaction powers that affect combat.
● Set-Specific Utility Powers. All noncombat, movement, sensory, survival, and support powers
tied to this Power Set.
● Enhancements. Common Enhancements appropriate to this Power Set with their specific
effects.
● Limitations. Common Limitations players often pair with this Power Set and what each restricts.

Chapter Twelve · General Utility Powers
General Utility Powers
Standalone powers for customizing your hero beyond your primary Power Sets. Minor superhuman
capabilities, biological traits, sensory enhancements, and niche abilities — each costing 1 Power Pick.
General Utility Powers are standalone powers that let you customize your hero beyond your primary
Power Sets. They represent minor superhuman capabilities, biological traits, sensory enhancements, and
niche abilities that fit a hero's concept without requiring full Power Set investment. Each General Utility
Power costs 1 Power Pick and is available according to your Campaign Rank and Tier limits.
How General Utilities Interact with Power Sets
General Utility Powers do not replace Power Sets. If a dedicated Power Set already covers a capability as
a Core Track benefit, taking the General Utility version provides a lighter expression of that capability —
not an equivalent one. Where a General Utility overlaps with a Power Set, the entry notes it explicitly.
A hero who takes a General Utility version of an ability and later invests in the corresponding Power Set
should use the Power Set's superior version. The General Utility does not provide additional benefit on
top of the Power Set.
The passive HP recovery stacking rule from Playing the Game applies throughout: passive per-Turn HP
recovery from multiple sources uses the highest value only and does not stack.

Improvised Weapons
Anything that is not a weapon but used as one — a fire extinguisher, a chair, a section of pipe, a car door.

Armor and Protection
Most heroes do not wear traditional armor. Their protection comes from Powers, Class features, and
Active Defense rolls. For heroes who do wear armor, it provides a flat bonus to one specific Active
Defense roll result rather than replacing the Active Defense system.
How Armor Works
Armor applies after the Active Defense roll is made. If a hero rolls their Parry/Block defense and their
armor provides a +2 bonus, that bonus is added to their rolled total before comparing to the attacker's roll.

Invention and Crafting
Some heroes do not just use gear. They build it. HEROIC 5e uses two modes of invention: Downtime
Invention for planned creation with proper resources and time, and Kitbashing for improvised field
assembly when there is no time and no guarantee of safety.
Both modes use the Technology Skill and the standard difficulty scale. Both use Degrees of Success to
determine the quality of the result. Neither produces permanent Power Set effects without the appropriate
Power Set and Origin to support it.
Who Can Invent
● Any hero with Technology trained can attempt Downtime Invention.
● Kitbashing requires Technology trained and is significantly harder without a relevant Specialty
such as Electronics, Power Armor, or Security Systems.
● A hero's Origin and Power Sets determine what they can build. A Super-Science hero can invent
devices that replicate effects from their existing Power Sets up to their current Tier. A Gearbound
hero can upgrade and modify their existing gear. A hero without a relevant Origin or Power Set
can build mundane technological devices but cannot build devices that replicate Power effects.

Bonus Actions
You only have a Bonus Action when a rule, Power, Talent, or Class feature gives you something to do
with it. You cannot spend a Bonus Action on nothing.
Reactions
A Reaction is a response to a specific trigger. You normally have 1 Reaction per round. A Reaction resets
at the start of your Turn. A Reaction interrupts the triggering event unless the specific rule says otherwise.
Secondary Attacks
Unless a rule explicitly states otherwise, all secondary attacks and granted actions are made at
Disadvantage. This includes bonus attacks, Reaction attacks, granted attacks, follow-up attacks, chain
attacks, assist attacks, and extra attacks created by Team Combos.
The Ready Action
When you take the Ready action, choose an action and describe a trigger in one sentence. The trigger
must be a specific observable event, such as "when an enemy moves within Close range of me" or "when
an ally is hit by an attack." Broad triggers like "when anything happens" are not valid.
When the trigger occurs before the start of your next Turn, you may fire your readied action as a
Reaction. If the trigger does not occur, the readied action is lost. A readied Encounter Power expends the
resource whether or not the trigger fires. Daily Powers cannot be readied. At-Will Powers may be readied
freely.

Respec
At Levels 3, 6, and 9, a hero may choose to Respec — an optional decision made at the same time as the

Origin, Class, and Ability Scores do not change through a Respec. These are identity. Everything

● Powers — any Power Pick purchases, including Core Track steps, Branch Powers, Utility

● Limitations — may be added, removed, or swapped, subject to Campaign Rank caps.
● Skills and Specialties — any Skill training or Specialties gained at character creation or through
advancement picks.

Every compelling villain has a line they will not cross, and that line reveals character. The line creates the

4. What is their relationship to the heroes?
Do they know them? Respect them? Feel contempt? Want something from them specifically? A villain
who has no opinion about the heroes has not noticed the most interesting thing in their world.
5. What happens when they lose?
Not defeat in the final sense — setback. When the heroes disrupt a plan, how does the villain respond?
Do they adapt? Escalate? Retreat and regroup? This question is your session-to-session campaign
engine.
Using the Full Build
Once you have answered those questions, build the villain as a full character using the same rules
players use. Choose an Origin that fits their background. Choose a Class that reflects how they act under
pressure: a villain who controls through information and manipulation is a Mastermind. One who
overwhelms through sheer force is a Bruiser. One who makes everyone around them feel something is a
Beacon. Choose Power Sets based on their concept, not based on what would be mechanically
challenging to fight.
Apply Boss Rules from Chapter 15: Resolve (once per encounter, ignore a condition) and Condition Cap
(maximum two simultaneous conditions). Give them a Flaw — not a mechanical limitation from the Power
Sets chapter, but a Merits and Flaws chapter Flaw that creates story. The heroes who discover and
exploit that Flaw have earned their victory.

Minions do not have Power Tiers in the traditional sense — their danger comes from numbers and

Named NPCs
Named NPCs are specific individuals with identities, histories, and roles in the story that extend beyond a
single encounter. They have full stat blocks, defined Power Sets, and Merits and Flaws that make them
feel like people rather than obstacles. They have HP. They can return. They carry Flaws the heroes can
discover and exploit.
Named NPCs can be any Power Tier. A Low Power Named NPC might be a recurring street-level contact
who occasionally needs rescuing — or who occasionally pulls the heroes out of trouble. A Cosmic Power
Named NPC is a campaign-defining figure whose presence reshapes the story.
Boss
Any Named NPC can be designated as the Boss of a specific encounter. This is a per-encounter
designation, not a permanent trait. The same villain is a Boss in the climactic confrontation at the end of
Act Two and a standard Named NPC in the brief appearance where they escape mid-session.
