Chapter Thirteen · Equipment & Gear
Equipment & Gear
Weapons, armor, gadgets, vehicles, invention, and crafting. Equipment rules should never slow the table
down — they should create interesting decisions, dramatic moments, and occasionally spectacular
failures.
Heroes punch through walls, fire energy blasts, fly at supersonic speeds, and reshape the battlefield with
their minds. Equipment exists alongside all of that. It does not replace it.
Gear is the physical layer of a hero's life: the tools they carry, the vehicles they drive, the weapons they
use when powers are not enough or not appropriate, and for some heroes, the devices they build that
make them who they are. A billionaire in a powered suit is defined as much by their engineering genius as
by what the suit can do. A street-level detective's lockpicks and scanner are as important to them as any
power. A legacy hero's inherited equipment carries the weight of history.
This chapter covers everything a hero might own, use, build, or lose. For groups who want detailed
resource tracking, the Resources and Wealth section at the end of this chapter provides an optional
system for handling money, purchasing power, and the practical economics of heroic life. Groups who
prefer narrative resource handling use the Cash Flow, Wealth, and Patron Merits from Chapter 9 and do
not need anything from that section.
The Role of Gear
What Gear Is — and Is Not
Gear is not a Power Set. A hero who builds a sonic cannon from spare parts is using Technology,
invention, and engineering genius. The sonic cannon is the result of those things expressed as
equipment.
Gear is not a Talent. Talents represent trained edges and signature techniques. A hero with the
Marksman Talent shoots better. The gun they are shooting is gear.
Gear is not a Merit. Cash Flow covers financial resources. The Vehicle Merit covers owning a significant
vehicle. The Base of Operations Merit covers having a headquarters. Those Merits establish that things
exist. This chapter covers how those things work mechanically.
Gear provides three things: access to situations the hero could not otherwise reach, utility solutions to
problems that powers and skills alone cannot address, and capability expressions that fit the hero's
concept without requiring a full Power Set purchase.
The line between gear and Powers matters when it comes to the Gear Dependent Flaw. A hero whose
sonic cannon is a convenience carries it because it helps. A hero whose sonic cannon is the source of
their primary offensive capability needs the Gear Dependent Flaw to reflect what happens when it is taken

away. If losing a piece of equipment would significantly reduce the hero's effectiveness in their core role, it
should carry the Gear Dependent Flaw at the appropriate Rating.
Standard Equipment
Standard equipment is anything a hero might reasonably carry without special access, wealth, or a Merit
to justify it. The GM should not require a roll or a resource check for a hero to have a flashlight, a first aid
kit, a charged phone, or appropriate clothing for their concept.
The Rule of Thumb
If a reasonable person in the hero's position would have it, they have it. A street-level detective has a
notebook, a camera, and a burner phone. A corporate hero has a tailored suit and an expense account.
An alien hero has whatever their Origin package specifies.
When a hero wants something that is not obvious from their concept, the GM applies one of three rulings:
they simply have it because it makes sense, they can get it with a Prepared Cache Merit use, or they
need to seek it out through play, a Merit, or a Resource check if using the optional rules.
Standard equipment never produces Power-level effects. A hero cannot assert they have a portable EMP
device that shuts down all electronics in a building just because they have a Technology Specialty. That is
invention territory, not standard equipment.
Weapons and Combat Equipment
Most heroes in HEROIC 5e do not rely on weapons as their primary combat expression. Powers, Class
features, and martial training carry the offensive load. When a hero does use a weapon, it fits into one of
four categories.
Unarmed
Punches, kicks, grapples, headbutts, and anything the hero does with their body.
Attack: STR or FIG
Damage: 1 Power Die (no modifiers unless a Class feature or Power increases it)
The Martial Artist Talent improves unarmed damage dice and opens additional options.
Light Weapons
Knives, batons, collapsible weapons, small firearms, and anything that can be concealed or used quickly.
Attack: DEX or FIG
Damage: 1 Power Die
May carry the Concealable or Thrown property.

Improvised Weapons
Anything that is not a weapon but used as one — a fire extinguisher, a chair, a section of pipe, a car door.
Improvised weapons deal 1 Power Die of damage and have no special properties unless the fiction
supports one. The Improvised Fighter Talent significantly improves this option.
Armor and Protection
Most heroes do not wear traditional armor. Their protection comes from Powers, Class features, and
Active Defense rolls. For heroes who do wear armor, it provides a flat bonus to one specific Active
Defense roll result rather than replacing the Active Defense system.
How Armor Works
Armor applies after the Active Defense roll is made. If a hero rolls their Parry/Block defense and their
armor provides a +2 bonus, that bonus is added to their rolled total before comparing to the attacker's roll.
Light Armor
Reinforced clothing, tactical vests, thin ballistic weave.
Bonus: +2 to Parry/Block or Dodge (choose when acquired)
Does not restrict movement or impose penalties. Concealable under loose clothing with a successful
Stealth check.
Heavy Armor
Full tactical gear, ballistic plate carriers, medieval-style armor, dense environmental suits.
Bonus: +4 to Parry/Block or Dodge (choose when acquired)
Imposes Disadvantage on Acrobatics and Stealth checks. Not Concealable.
Powered Armor (as Gear)
A commercially available exoskeleton or military powered suit has the following profile:
● +3 to Parry/Block and Dodge simultaneously
● Built-in strength enhancement: +2 to STR-based checks while wearing
● Specific built-in features defined at acquisition with GM approval (integrated communications,
environmental sealing, enhanced sensors, etc.)
Powered Armor vs. the Armor Systems Power Set
A hero whose powered armor is their primary Power Set expression is using the Armor Systems Power
Set from Chapter 11. That armor is a Power Set, not equipment, and does not use the rules in this
section.

The distinction: if the armor could theoretically be removed and given to another character who would
benefit from it equally, it is gear. If the armor is an expression of the hero's unique genius, cannot be
easily replicated, and losing it would remove their primary powers, it is a Power Set with the Gear
Dependent Flaw.
Gadgets and Specialist Equipment
Gadgets are single-use or limited-use items that produce specific effects — smoke grenades, sensor
drones, medical injectors, grappling lines, signal jammers, and specialized restraints that populate a
hero's utility belt.
Gadgets never directly replicate Power Set effects. A smoke grenade does not replicate the Concealment
power. It creates smoke, which is an environmental effect the GM adjudicates based on the fiction. A
specialized restraint does not replicate Telekinetic Bind. It physically restrains a target, which requires the
target to be cooperative or already subdued.
Using Gadgets
● Simple gadgets activate with a Free Object Interaction or Bonus Action.
● Complex gadgets require an Action and sometimes a Technology or relevant Skill check. The
GM sets the DC using the standard difficulty scale based on the complexity of the task and the
pressure of the situation.
● Gadgets used in combat that require targeting use the same attack and defense resolution as
Powers: roll an attack, the defender rolls Active Defense, compare results, apply Degrees of
Success.
Acquiring Gadgets
● Standard gadgets: available through Cash Flow, standard equipment assertion, or the Prepared
Cache Merit.
● Restricted or military-grade gadgets: require Legal Authority, Connected, Patron, or Gear
Access at an appropriate Rating.
● Custom or unique gadgets: require the Invention rules below.
The Prepared Cache Merit and Gadgets
A hero with Prepared Cache 1 can declare they previously stored one gadget in a plausible nearby
location once per session. This covers the classic superhero moment of "I stashed an EMP device here
last week just in case." The GM may reject items that are implausible given the hero's concept or the
location.
Vehicles
Vehicles are significant equipment. A hero who owns a notable vehicle should have the Vehicle Merit at
the appropriate Rating. A hero who drives a car they borrowed or commandeered does not need the Merit
for that one scene.
Vehicle Ratings
All vehicles have three ratings that govern their mechanical expression in play.

Specific capabilities defined when the vehicle is acquired. A motorcycle has none. An advanced pursuit

● Stealth Systems: Disadvantage on checks to detect or track it.
● Pursuit Systems: Advantage on Vehicles checks during chases.
● Ejection Systems: The hero can exit safely at any speed.
Special features are negotiated with the GM at acquisition and should reflect the vehicle's concept and
the hero's resources.

Vehicles and Zone Movement
A vehicle moves according to its Speed Rating during combat and chase scenes. A hero piloting a vehicle
uses their Vehicles Skill for maneuvers. Standard movement and Dash apply: a vehicle can move its
Speed Rating in Zones as a standard move, or double that on a Dash.
Vehicle Combat
When heroes fight from, on, or against vehicles:
● Attacking a vehicle targets its Armor Rating using normal attack and defense resolution.
● The vehicle's Armor Rating does not roll Active Defense — attacks against vehicles compare the
attacker's roll against a static Defense Value of 10 + Armor Rating + pilot's Prowess.
● Vehicles do not take conditions. They take hits. When a vehicle reaches 0 hits it is disabled or
destroyed, determined by the fiction and the GM.
● A hero attacking from inside or on top of a moving vehicle rolls attacks with Disadvantage unless
they have a Vehicles Specialty or a relevant Class feature or Talent that removes the penalty.
● A hero attacking a moving vehicle from outside rolls attacks with Disadvantage unless the
vehicle is stationary or they have a relevant Talent.
Pursuit Rules
A chase is a structured encounter using the Zone system. The GM defines the starting Zone positions of
pursuer and quarry. Each Turn, both sides take actions using the Vehicles Skill or relevant movement
Powers.
The goal for the pursuer is to reduce the distance to Close range and maintain it. The goal for the quarry
is to increase the distance to Long range or beyond and maintain it, or reach a specific escape point.
Chase Action Mechanic
Push Attempt to increase or decrease distance by 1
Zone. Vehicles check DC 15.
Maneuver Attempt to force the other party into difficult terrain
or a hazard. Opposed Vehicles check.
Hazard Introduce an environmental complication. Vehicles
check DC 15 for the other party to navigate.
Attack Make a standard attack while maintaining speed,
at Disadvantage unless a relevant Talent removes

Degrees of Success in Chases
A Strong Success on a Push gives the acting party 2 Zones of movement instead of 1.
A HEROIC Success produces a Momentum Effect: the pursuer gets Advantage on their next check, or
the quarry loses a Turn of movement from a collision or near-miss.

Invention and Crafting
Some heroes do not just use gear. They build it. HEROIC 5e uses two modes of invention: Downtime
Invention for planned creation with proper resources and time, and Kitbashing for improvised field
assembly when there is no time and no guarantee of safety.
Both modes use the Technology Skill and the standard difficulty scale. Both use Degrees of Success to
determine the quality of the result. Neither produces permanent Power Set effects without the appropriate
Power Set and Origin to support it.
Who Can Invent
● Any hero with Technology trained can attempt Downtime Invention.
● Kitbashing requires Technology trained and is significantly harder without a relevant Specialty
such as Electronics, Power Armor, or Security Systems.
● A hero's Origin and Power Sets determine what they can build. A Super-Science hero can invent
devices that replicate effects from their existing Power Sets up to their current Tier. A Gearbound
hero can upgrade and modify their existing gear. A hero without a relevant Origin or Power Set
can build mundane technological devices but cannot build devices that replicate Power effects.
Downtime Invention
During Downtime, a hero with Technology trained may attempt to build a device. The process has four
steps.
Step 1: Define the Device
The player describes what the device does, what effect it replicates (if any), and what Power Set and Tier
it draws from. The GM approves the concept or suggests modifications.
Step 2: Determine Resources
The GM determines what materials are needed based on the device's complexity. Resources are either:
● Available: the hero has the appropriate Cash Flow, Wealth, or Patron to cover them without a
check.
● Limited: the hero must make a Resource check or narrative effort to acquire them.
● Unavailable: the materials cannot be found without a significant story arc.
Step 3: Make the Technology Check
Downtime Invention DCs
15Simple utility device with a known effect
20Complex device replicating an Encounter-tier Power effect

Invented Gear and the Gear Dependent Flaw
If a hero builds a device that becomes central to their heroic identity and primary combat effectiveness,
the Gear Dependent Flaw applies at the Rating appropriate to how devastating its loss would be. A hero
who builds a utility scanner they use occasionally does not need the Flaw. A hero whose entire offensive
capability comes from a self-built energy projector should take Gear Dependent at Rating 2 or 3.
Kitbashing
Kitbashing is invention under pressure. No proper workspace, no guaranteed materials, no time to do it
right. It is the hero pulling a device apart and rebuilding it into something new in the middle of a collapsing
building because there is no other option.
Kitbashing requires available materials. The GM determines whether the scene contains materials
appropriate to what the hero is attempting. A hero cannot Kitbash a sonic emitter in an empty room. A
hero absolutely can Kitbash a signal jammer from a broken drone, a phone, and a fire suppression
system panel.

Worked Examples
Example — Downtime Invention
Ghostwork (Super-Science Origin, Technology trained with Expertise, Electronics Specialty) wants to
build a miniaturized sensor drone during Downtime. She and the GM agree the drone replicates an At-Will

surveillance effect from her existing Power Set at her current Tier. Materials are available through her
Patron Merit.
She rolls Technology with Expertise: 1d20 + INT +4 + double Prowess (+6) = 1d20 + 10. She rolls 14.
Total: 24. The DC was 15 for a straightforward At-Will device. Strong Success by a margin of 9.
The drone works as intended with Prowess uses, and Ghostwork chooses the additional benefit: the
drone is small enough to be Concealable in a coat pocket.
Example — Kitbashing
Crashpoint (Guardian, Technology untrained) is trapped in a power substation with a villain controlling
the electrical systems. She cannot build anything sophisticated — the GM rules Kitbashing is not
available to her here, as she lacks Technology training entirely.
Later, Ghostwork arrives. She assesses the materials (moderate quality), the complexity (simple: she
wants to short-circuit the villain's control interface, an At-Will equivalent effect), and the time pressure
(high: the villain is active). The GM sets DC 20.
Ghostwork rolls Technology with Expertise: 1d20 + 10. She rolls 11. Total: 21. Normal Success. The
improvised short-circuit works once. The villain's control interface goes dark for one round. The heroes
use the opening.
Gear and Origins
Different Origins interact with gear in different ways.
Gearbound
A Gearbound hero derives their primary powers from equipment. Their gear is their Power Set. The Gear
Dependent Flaw applies at Rating 2 or 3 for primary gear. A Gearbound hero who loses their primary
equipment loses their Power Set access until the gear is recovered or rebuilt.
A Gearbound hero has natural access to Downtime Invention for their own gear and may Kitbash
modifications to their existing equipment at lower DCs: reduce the Kitbashing DC by 3 when modifying
their own established gear.
Super-Science
A Super-Science hero invents things. Their Power Sets represent the categories of technology they have
mastered. Downtime Invention is a core part of their concept and they should be encouraged to use it
regularly.
A Super-Science hero may build devices for other heroes during Downtime with GM approval: the
receiving hero must have the Gear Access Merit or pay a Resource Cost appropriate to the device's tier
to represent the materials.

Legacy
A Legacy hero may have inherited significant equipment from a predecessor. This equipment is defined at
character creation with GM approval and functions as established gear with the appropriate Vehicle or
Gear Access Merit already built into the Origin package. Legacy equipment that is central to the heroic
identity carries the Gear Dependent Flaw.
Legacy equipment can be upgraded through Downtime Invention if the hero has Technology trained and
the relevant expertise.
Alien
An alien hero may carry technology that is beyond current Earth manufacturing capability. Alien gear
functions as described in this chapter but cannot be repaired using the Technology Skill alone :
repairing alien technology requires either the alien hero themselves, a specific alien resource, or a
Technology check at DC 25. An alien hero with Technology trained can repair their own equipment
normally.
Losing, Repairing, and Upgrading Gear
Losing Gear
Gear can be destroyed in combat, stolen, confiscated by authorities, or simply lost in the chaos of a
desperate fight. The GM introduces gear loss as a narrative consequence of combat damage, villain
action, or dramatic failure.
● When a hero loses gear that carries the Gear Dependent Flaw, the Flaw activates at its full Rating
until the gear is recovered or replaced.
● When a hero loses gear that does not carry the Flaw, they lose access to that item's benefits until
it is recovered or replaced. No Flaw activates but the practical loss still matters.

Upgrading Gear
Existing gear can be upgraded during Downtime using the Invention rules. Upgrading existing gear is
easier than building from scratch: reduce the DC by 3 when modifying a piece of gear the hero already
owns and understands. The upgrade must represent a plausible improvement to the existing item, not a
wholesale replacement.
A hero who upgrades gear through play may also improve the Vehicle Merit or Gear Access Merit
associated with that gear during an advancement choice, with GM approval and story justification. The
fiction must support it: a hero who has spent three story arcs improving their vehicle has earned the right
to call it Rating 3 without simply paying an advancement cost.
Resources and Wealth (Optional Rules)
Using This Section
The default resource system in HEROIC 5e is narrative. The Cash Flow, Wealth, and Patron Merits from
Chapter 9 establish what a hero can reasonably access. The GM interprets these Merits to determine
what the hero can acquire without tracking individual purchases.
Groups who want more granular resource tracking may use the following optional system. If your group is
not using this system, the Merits are sufficient and this section can be ignored entirely.

Every hero using this system has a Resource Rating from 0 to 5. This rating is derived from their Merits
and story circumstances. It is not fixed: it can rise or fall with story events.

Resource Cost
Every significant purchase has a Resource Cost from 0 to 5.
● If the hero's Resource Rating meets or exceeds the Resource Cost, the purchase happens
without a roll. They simply have it.
● If the Resource Cost exceeds the hero's Rating by 1 , they may attempt a Cash Flow or
Influence check against DC 15 to stretch their resources. Success means they acquire it with
some effort. Failure means they cannot manage it this session but may try again later.
● If the Resource Cost exceeds the hero's Rating by 2 or more , the purchase is beyond their
means without GM approval and a significant story justification — a major heist, a windfall, or a
generous ally.

Resource Rating Fluctuation
Resource Ratings are not permanent. Story events change them. A hero who is defrauded, whose assets
are frozen, or whose Patron withdraws support may drop a Rating level temporarily or permanently. A
hero who discovers a cache of alien technology, inherits a fortune, or wins a major lawsuit may gain a
Rating level as a campaign reward.
Resource Rating changes require GM approval and story justification. They are not advancement
choices. They are story consequences.

A Note on Group Resources
When the party operates as a team with shared infrastructure — a Base of Operations or Team
Membership Merit — the group's combined resources may be treated as a shared Resource Rating for
mission-specific purposes. The GM determines how the group's resources combine based on their Merits.
A team with one billionaire member (Resource Rating 5) and three street-level heroes (Resource Rating
1) has access to Rating 5 resources for team operations, but individual heroes still have their personal
Rating for personal purchases.
