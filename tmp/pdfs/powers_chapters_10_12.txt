Powers
The superhero engine. The framework behind every Power Set: how they are structured, how the
economy works, how they advance, and what the rules mean.

Powers are the superhero engine of HEROIC 5e.
Everything else in this game defines who the hero is: where they came from, how they act under
pressure, what they have learned, who they know, and what complications follow them through life.
Powers define what they can actually do that no ordinary human can. The energy blast. The impossible
speed. The mind that reads thoughts and rewrites memories. The body that shrugs off tank shells. The
will that commands weather systems. The fist that moves mountains.
Powers are not spells. A hero does not run out of being a superhero. An energy projector can always
project energy. A telepath can always sense surface thoughts. A speedster can always run faster than
anything else on the road. That baseline capability is always available because it is part of who the hero
is, not a resource they manage. Encounter Powers and Daily Powers represent the hero pushing beyond
their baseline — going harder and farther than they normally would, producing the splash-page moments
that define superhero fiction.

This chapter covers everything about the Powers system: structure, Power Picks, Power types, the attack
and Effect Value distinction, Enhancements, Limitations, Power Stunts, and advancement. The Power
Sets themselves are in Chapter 11. Read this framework first — once the language is clear, every Power
Set entry is fast to read and easy to use at the table.
Design Principles
The Powers system follows these principles without exception.
● Powers are superhero-native. Built for comic-book action, not fantasy spellcasting repainted in
capes. A fire controller does not prepare fire spells and manage spell slots. They control fire. A
telepath is not expending mental energy on every surface read. They are psychic. Powers
express who the hero is.
● Heroes feel complete at Level 1. Campaign Rank defines power scale. A World Class hero
beginning play at Level 1 already feels World Class. Level measures experience and refinement.
Campaign Rank measures the scale of fiction the heroes belong to.
● No Power Points. HEROIC 5e does not use resource pools to fuel Powers. At-Will Powers are
always available. Encounter Powers refresh each encounter. Daily Powers are available once per
day safely. The Edge economy handles dramatic pushes beyond that baseline.
● Clear mechanics. Powers use attack rolls, saves, action types, Advantage, Disadvantage,
Prowess, durations, and conditions. Every power entry states exactly what it does. No power
relies on vague language that requires interpretation at the table.
● Concept chooses expression. The same Power Set can look completely different depending on
the hero's Origin, Class, Governing Ability, and story. Two heroes with Energy Generation can
express the same mechanical framework as a solar-powered alien and a technological genius in
a suit. The mechanics are shared. The identity is personal.
Attributes and Powers
Attributes define the person. Powers define the extraordinary capability layered on top of that person. A
hero with Super Strength does not automatically need high Strength. A telepath does not automatically
need high Intelligence. The Attribute describes the hero's personal quality. The Power Set describes their
superhuman capability.
Each Power Set has a Governing Ability that drives its attack rolls and Effect Values — representing
how the hero controls, channels, or expresses that Power Set.

Power Sets and Core Tracks
A Power Set is the family of related abilities a hero can access: Energy Generation, Super Strength,
Telepathy, Shadow Control, and so on. Every significant Power Set has a Core Track — the spine of the
Power Set that defines the hero's scale, access, and identity within that set.
Core Track steps are purchased in order. You cannot take Core Track 2 without Core Track 1.
Step Role
Core Track 1 Basic expression. Tier 1 access. Establishes the
Power Set.
Core Track 2 Stronger expression. Tier 2 access. Expands
scale.
Core Track 3 World Class expression. Tier 3 access. Full power
identity.
Core Track 4 Apex expression. Tier 3 capstone power. The
pinnacle of this Power Set.

Eight Sets That Add to Their Governing Ability Score

force they control. These sets add +1 to their governing Ability Score at each Core Track step. A hero

Branch Powers
Branch Powers are the specific things a hero can do with a Power Set — attacks, defenses, Reactions,
battlefield control effects, movement options, Sustained effects, transformations, and dramatic scene
powers. Every Branch Power has all of the following elements stated explicitly in its entry. No power relies
on implication or assumed defaults.
● Name. What the power is called on the character sheet.
● Tier and Type. The Tier (1, 2, 3, or Apex) and the Type (At-Will, Encounter, Daily, Utility, Passive,
or Sustained).
● Action cost. Action, Bonus Action, Reaction, Movement, No Action, or Passive.
● Prerequisite. Usually a Core Track step. Some powers require other powers first.
● Range or Area. Stated in range bands with foot equivalents in parentheses.
● Roll or Save. Whether the power uses an attack roll, a save, both, or neither.
● Damage or Effect. The exact mechanical result. Never vague.
● Duration. When the effect ends. Every power that applies a condition states the duration
explicitly. The default condition duration is until the end of the attacker's next Turn unless the
entry says otherwise.
● Degrees of Success riders. Normal Success, Strong Success, and HEROIC Success effects, if
applicable.
Standard Power Entry Format Power Name (Tier X Type, Action Cost)
*Prerequisite: Core Track step or other requirement
Rules text describing what the power does.
Range: Range band (foot equivalent).
Attack: 1d20 + governing attack modifier + Prowess vs. Active Defense. —OR—
Save: 1d20 + Attribute + Prowess vs. Power Effect Value.
Hit / Failure: Exact damage and effect.
Strong Success: Additional effect if applicable.
HEROIC Success: Additional effect if applicable.
Duration: When any ongoing effect ends.
Power Types
Every power belongs to one of six types. The type determines how often it can be used and what it costs.
At-Will
Available every Turn without limit. These never run out because they are who the hero is. A fire controller
throws flames every Turn if they want to.
150

--- PDF PAGE 155 ---
Encounter
Once per encounter, refreshing when the encounter ends. Represents the hero going harder than their
baseline — reliable within a fight, not infinite.
Daily
Once per day safely, refreshing on Downtime. The splash-page moments. A hero can push beyond safe
use by spending 2 Edge and gaining 1 Burnout.
Utility
Noncombat capabilities: movement, sensory, survival, communication, investigation, support. Available
freely unless the entry specifies otherwise.
Passive
Always active. No action required. No declaration. The benefit simply exists as a constant fact about the
hero.
Sustained
Active as long as maintained. Sustaining costs a Bonus Action at the start of each Turn. A hero may
sustain only one Sustained Power at a time. Ends if the hero is Stunned, Incapacitated, or Unconscious.
Power Attack Roll vs. Effect Value
The attack roll and the Effect Value are two separate things. Do not conflate them.
The attack roll determines whether the effect lands. It is always governed by the delivery method: FIG
for melee, DEX for ranged, INT for mental, CHA for social. → Chapter 1: Governing Attributes
The Effect Value determines whether the secondary consequence is resisted. It is governed by the
Governing Ability of the Power Set that produced the effect.
Attack Roll = 1d20 + delivery modifier (FIG/DEX/INT/CHA) + Prowess
Effect Value = 10 + Power Set's Governing Ability modifier + Prowess
Worked Example
Gyro fires a Bio-Kinetic Blast (ranged). He rolls DEX + Prowess to hit against the target's Active Defense.
If the attack lands, the target resists the Daze effect against Gyro's Effect Value of 10 + STR + Prowess.
Two different attributes. Two different questions. Both clean. The delivery was ranged so DEX governs
the to-hit. The effect is a physical force from his Super Strength Power Set so STR governs the Effect
Value.

Effect Values for Class Features
Every Class Feature that generates a resisted effect is tied to that Class's primary attribute, always. A
multiclassed character may have multiple Effect Values in play — one per class in their build. The source
of the effect determines which applies. Every Class Feature that generates a resisted effect must declare
its Governing Ability explicitly in the feature header.
Campaign Rank and Powers
Campaign Rank determines the maximum number of Power Sets the hero can have, the maximum Tier of
powers they can access, and the number of Starting Power Picks at character creation.
Campaign Rank Max Power Sets Max Tier Starting Power Picks
Street Level 3 Tier 1 8

Campaign Rank is set at the start of the campaign and defines the scale of superhero fiction the table is
playing. It is not a character advancement milestone. A Level 1 World Class hero is already operating at
World Class scale. A Level 10 Street Level hero is still a street-level hero, just a highly experienced one.
Tier Access During Play
● Street Level heroes may purchase Tier 2 powers from Level 5 through play only — never at
character creation.
● Mid-Level heroes may purchase Tier 3 powers from Level 5 through play only — never at
character creation.
● World Class has no Tier restriction beyond Campaign Rank maximums.

Minimum Baseline at Level 1
Every hero at Level 1 should have: at least one Core Track step establishing their primary Power Set, at
least two At-Will Powers giving reliable combat and utility expression, and at least one Encounter Power
for high-pressure moments. A Level 1 hero should already feel complete. Not a hero in training. Not a
hero waiting for their powers to develop. A hero.
Advancement Picks
Power Picks are gained at every level from 2 to 10 — nine picks across the full ten-level game, in addition
to Starting Power Picks at character creation.

Advancement Cost
All Picks Cost 1 Pick at Advancement
All Power Pick purchases at advancement cost 1 Pick for 1 Power, regardless of Tier. A Tier 3 power
costs 1 advancement Pick. This is the built-in reward for growth through play — the economy that made a
Tier 3 power expensive at character creation reflects how powerful that level is before a hero has grown
into it.
Power Dice
Powers use Power Dice for damage, healing, and certain effects. The die type is determined by
Campaign Rank — not by the Tier of the power. A Street Level hero who accesses a Tier 2 power through
play still uses d8 Power Dice, not d10.
Campaign Rank Power Die
Street Level d8
Mid-Level d10
World Class d12
The individual power entry specifies how many Power Dice are rolled. These numbers are set in the
power entry and do not scale automatically with level unless the entry specifically says they do.
Enhancements
Enhancements are small improvements to a power, Utility, or Core Track the hero already possesses.
They make existing powers more useful, precise, versatile, or reliable without being new powers in their
own right. One Power Pick buys two Enhancements, always purchased in pairs.
Enhancements may provide the following types of improvements. The specific enhancement chosen must
be plausible given the power it modifies and the hero's concept.
● Range. Extends the power's effective range by one range band.
● Targeting. Allows the power to affect one additional adjacent target.
● Protection. Extends a defensive power to cover an ally within Close range.
● Duration. Extends a Utility's effect from one minute to one scene, or from one scene to one
encounter.
154

--- PDF PAGE 159 ---
● Precision. Allows a normally destructive power to be used nonlethally without changing its
mechanical effect.
● Concealment. Allows a normally obvious power signature to be masked, muted, or hidden from
casual observation.
● Action Economy. Allows a Utility to be activated as a Bonus Action instead of an Action.
● Reliability. Grants Advantage on a power's attack roll in a specific, narrow circumstance defined
when the Enhancement is taken.
Enhancement Limits
A hero may not use Enhancements to exceed Campaign Rank, access Tier 3 effects during a Street
Level campaign, permanently remove a Limitation, or transform a Utility into a full Branch Power.
Enhancements are refinements, not replacements for the Power Pick economy.
Limitations
Limitations are meaningful restrictions attached to a Power Set, Core Track, or specific power expression.
Each Limitation grants +1 Power Pick when taken.
Limitations are not general life complications or story hooks — those belong to Merits and Flaws. A
Limitation must directly and specifically restrict how a power works at the table. A Limitation that never
meaningfully fires in play is not a Limitation. It is free points.

Power Stunts
A Power Stunt is an improvised use of a Power Set the hero already possesses — attempting something
the hero has not formally purchased but that falls within the thematic space of their powers.
Spending 1 Edge allows the hero to attempt a Power Stunt. The GM assigns the action cost, the roll
required, the effect, and any associated risk or Burnout trigger. The Power Stunt is always weaker,
shorter, riskier, or more conditional than the equivalent purchased power would be.
Power Stunts: What They Are and Are Not
● A Power Stunt should never become a permanent free power. It represents improvising at the
edge of capability, not discovering a power the hero secretly had all along.
156

--- PDF PAGE 161 ---
● Power Stunts work within the thematic space of an existing Power Set only. A Fire Control hero
can attempt to create a controlled fire vortex as a Power Stunt. They cannot attempt to generate
electricity — that is not within Fire Control's thematic space.
● If a Power Stunt fires successfully and the hero wants that capability permanently, they purchase
it with a Power Pick during advancement.
Common Power Stunt applications include: extending a power's normal use in an unusual direction,
producing a one-time variant effect, solving an environmental problem through creative power use,
creating a rescue opportunity through unconventional application, combining two owned Power Sets in a
dramatic moment, or reducing collateral damage through unusual power expression.
Power Stunt: Emulation
Emulation is a specific subset of the Power Stunt rules — its most demanding expression, not a separate
system. Emulation allows a hero to reach beyond what they have purchased and produce the full effect of
a specific unpurchased Branch Power within an owned Power Set. Where a standard Power Stunt
produces an improvised reduced-scale effect, Emulation forces the hero's powers to do something their
body is not yet built for — at full effect, at full cost.
Emulation is not improvisation. It is not a shortcut. It exists for the moment when the situation demands
something the hero cannot yet do and the player is willing to pay the real price.

Requirements
● Edge Cost. Spend 2 Edge before anything else resolves. If the hero does not have 2 Edge
available, Emulation cannot be attempted.
● Thematic Requirement. The Branch Power being Emulated must belong to a Power Set the
hero already owns and must be a Branch Power the hero has not purchased.
● Tier Cap. The Branch Power must be at or below the hero's current Tier access as permitted by
Campaign Rank. A hero cannot Emulate a Branch Power above their maximum Tier.

● Frequency. Once per Day. A hero who Emulates at any point during a session has spent that
option for the remainder of the Day, regardless of how many encounters follow.
● Action Cost. Emulation consumes the hero's Action and Bonus Action simultaneously. If either
has already been spent, Emulation cannot be attempted this Turn.
Procedure
1. Declaration. The player declares the specific unpurchased Branch Power by name. The GM
confirms it belongs to an owned Power Set, falls within Tier access, and has not been purchased.
2. Pay the Cost. Spend 2 Edge. This is spent before the power resolves.
3. Resolve the Power. The declared power fires using its full printed rules — attack roll, Effect
Value, damage dice, range, conditions, and duration exactly as written. No reduction. No
modification.
4. Make the Emulation Burnout Save. After the power resolves — whether it succeeded, missed,
or was resisted — the hero immediately makes the Emulation Burnout Save. No exception and
no delay.
The Emulation Burnout Save
1d20 + Governing Ability modifier + Prowess, at Disadvantage, vs. DC 20
Success: The hero gains 2 Burnout. The reach was extraordinary. The body registered it.
Failure: The hero's Burnout immediately becomes 5, regardless of current level. There is no version of
Emulation that costs nothing. The save determines whether the damage is severe or total — not whether
damage occurs at all.

Incapacitation Warning

A hero at Burnout 3 or higher who fails the Emulation Burnout Save does not simply land at Burnout 5.
Because a failure requires the hero to exceed Burnout 5, the sixth Burnout level is triggered — causing
immediate Incapacitation until their next Breather, per the standard Burnout rules. The math is not hidden.
The risk is the point.
Advancement Hook
When to Flag a Purchase
Emulation never becomes a permanent free capability. If a hero successfully Emulates the same Branch
Power twice across any number of sessions, the GM should flag it as a candidate for purchase at the
hero's next advancement. The power is not secretly theirs — their body has survived the reach twice.
That earns the right to buy it. It does not grant it.
Powers, Skills, and Talents
These three systems define different things. They should not replace one another.
● A hero who flies uses their Flight Power Set. A hero who pilots a jet uses their Vehicles Skill. A
hero who performs a precise aerobatic maneuver uses a Skill check, possibly with Advantage
from a relevant Talent.
● A telepath still benefits from the Insight Skill for reading people face to face. A speedster still
values Acrobatics for precise movement in tight spaces. A gadgeteer still needs Technology to
build and repair their kit.
● A hero who owns a jet uses the Vehicle Merit or Cash Flow Merit. A hero who builds a jetpack
uses Technology Skill. A hero whose flight is their public identity uses the Fame Merit.
Powers make the hero extraordinary. Skills make the hero competent. Talents make the hero sharp. All
three matter and none of them substitutes for the others.
Powers and Gear
When gear creates superhuman capability, build it using the Powers system. The gear is the narrative
explanation for where the power comes from. Mechanically it is still a Power Set. This keeps the game
balanced and prevents mundane equipment from replacing the Power Pick economy.
The Practical Distinction
● A grappling hook is gear. A web system that enables swinging, binding, rapid rescue, and
battlefield movement is a Power Set.
● A motorcycle is gear. A transforming attack-cycle capable of impossible pursuits, armored plating,
and mounted weapon systems is a Power Set and a Vehicle Merit.
● If a piece of gear creates superhuman capability, build it as Powers. If it provides access,
resources, or status, use the Gear chapter or the Merits chapter.
When gear is the source of a Power Set, the Gear Dependent Limitation and the Gear Dependent Flaw
both apply at the appropriate Rating. If the gear is taken away, the hero loses the Power Set until it is
recovered or rebuilt.

Reading Power Set Entries
Each Power Set entry in Chapter 11 follows a consistent format. Every entry opens with a description of
what the Power Set represents in superhero fiction and what kinds of heroes use it. The following
sections appear in every entry:
● Suggested Attributes. Which Attributes most commonly govern this Power Set and what each
choice represents. Some Power Sets have a fixed Governing Ability. Most offer two or three
options.
● Associated Conditions. The conditions this Power Set most commonly inflicts, exploits, or
resists — indicating its tactical role in combat.
● Default Damage Track. For Power Sets with a repeated attack pattern, the baseline formula for
unarmed strikes or attacks from this Power Set.
● Core Track. The four Core Track steps in order, each a passive benefit defining scale and
unlocking higher-Tier power access.
● Combat Powers. All At-Will, Encounter, Daily, and Reaction powers that affect combat.
● Set-Specific Utility Powers. All noncombat, movement, sensory, survival, and support powers
tied to this Power Set.
● Enhancements. Common Enhancements appropriate to this Power Set with their specific
effects.
● Limitations. Common Limitations players often pair with this Power Set and what each restricts.
A Note on Scale
Three Tiers, One Power Set
The Power Sets in Chapter 11 cover all three Campaign Ranks. Every Power Set has entries for all three
Tiers. A Street Level campaign uses only Tier 1 powers from each set. A Mid-Level campaign uses up to
Tier 2. A World Class campaign uses all three Tiers including Apex powers.
This means a hero who begins play at Street Level and whose campaign eventually elevates to World
Class can continue developing the same Power Set across the full ten levels. The hero who began
throwing small fireballs at Tier 1 is eventually hurling citywide firestorms at Tier 3. That progression is the
point.

Animal Powers is the Power Set of the evolutionary library — the biological capabilities of the entire
animal kingdom distilled into a single hero: striking speed, immense strength, echolocation, venom,
armored hide, and predatory awareness all available to one body that has learned to access four billion
years of biological development. Heroes may draw from genetic mutation, experimental enhancement,
alien biology, mystical animal totems, or a connection to the primal force of animal life itself.
Animal Powers heroes are defined by biological versatility. They move between capabilities as the
situation demands, and at higher tiers they can combine multiple animal capabilities simultaneously —

Always Use Only the Highest Value
Passive per-Turn HP recovery from multiple sources does not stack. If you have Regeneration and any

them together. This rule exists because the math breaks without it: combined passive healing at World

already powerful. It does not need to be combined with other passive healing to function.

Teleportation is the Power Set of instantaneous spatial displacement. Not movement. Not crossing
distance. Skipping it entirely. A teleporter does not travel from one point to another — they cease to exist
at the first point and begin to exist at the second, with the space between rendered meaningless. This
creates a relationship to the battlefield that neither flight nor super speed can replicate: a teleporter does
not need to cross the Zone between themselves and their target. They simply appear there.
DEX — physical instinct and combat precision, split-second acrobatic spatial awareness. INT —
calculated dimensional geometry and spatial mapping; super-science or techno-teleporter. WIS —
mystical attunement and dimensional sensitivity; portal-weaver navigating spaces between spaces
through spiritual awareness.

Creating New Power Sets
HEROIC 5e cannot possibly include every conceivable superpower without becoming an encyclopedia.
While the book provides dozens of fully realized Power Sets, your table will almost certainly want to
create something new — whether it is a highly specific concept or a fresh take on a classic idea.
This section gives players and GMs a clear, consistent framework to design balanced, playable Power
Sets that integrate seamlessly with the rest of the game.
Design Principles
305

--- PDF PAGE 310 ---
● Thematic Unity: All powers should feel like expressions of one central idea.
● Governing Ability: Every Power Set must have one primary governing Ability Score (STR, DEX,
CON, FIG, INT, WIS, CHA, or PER). This determines the set’s Effect Value.
● Power Die: Always use the campaign’s Power Die (d8 Street Level, d10 Mid-Level, d12 World
Class).
● Balance Target: New sets should feel roughly comparable in strength and versatility to published
sets such as Gravity Control, Energy Generation, or Super Strength.
● Playability: Powers must be fast and intuitive to resolve at the table.
Step-by-Step Creation Process
1. Define the Core Concept Write a single clear sentence that captures the essence of the
Power Set.
2. Choose the Governing Ability Select the Ability Score that best represents the set’s primary
expression.
3. Build the Core Track (usually 3–4 purchases) These represent increasing mastery and unlock
better powers.
4. Design At-Will Powers (2–4 options) Reliable abilities you can use every round.
5. Design Encounter Powers (2–4 options) Stronger, limited-use abilities with significant impact.
6. Design Daily / Apex Powers (1–2 options) Game-changing, high-cost abilities.
7. Design Utility Powers (2–4 options) Non-combat, exploration, or situational tools.
8. Create Enhancements (4–6 pairs) Improvements that can be added to existing powers (2
Enhancements = 1 Power Pick).
9. Optional: Thematic Limitations (2–4 options) Meaningful restrictions that grant +1 Power Pick
when taken.
Power Strength Guidelines
Tier Scope & Impact
Tier 1 Personal / street-level. Reliable but contained.
Tier 2 City / impressive scale. Strong area or sustained effects.
Tier 3 World / reality-bending scale. Dramatic, long-lasting, or massive effects.
Damage / Effect Baseline (adjust for theme):
● At-Will: 1–2 Power Dice + modifier
● Encounter: 2–3 Power Dice + modifier + strong condition
● Daily: 4+ Power Dice or major narrative effect
GM Approval
All new Power Sets require GM approval. Start conservative — it is easier to strengthen a weak set than
to nerf an overpowered one. Test new sets for 2–3 sessions before making them permanent.

General Utility Powers
Standalone powers for customizing your hero beyond your primary Power Sets. Minor superhuman
capabilities, biological traits, sensory enhancements, and niche abilities — each costing 1 Power Pick.
General Utility Powers are standalone powers that let you customize your hero beyond your primary
Power Sets. They represent minor superhuman capabilities, biological traits, sensory enhancements, and
niche abilities that fit a hero's concept without requiring full Power Set investment. Each General Utility
Power costs 1 Power Pick and is available according to your Campaign Rank and Tier limits.
How General Utilities Interact with Power Sets
General Utility Powers do not replace Power Sets. If a dedicated Power Set already covers a capability as
a Core Track benefit, taking the General Utility version provides a lighter expression of that capability —
not an equivalent one. Where a General Utility overlaps with a Power Set, the entry notes it explicitly.
A hero who takes a General Utility version of an ability and later invests in the corresponding Power Set
should use the Power Set's superior version. The General Utility does not provide additional benefit on
top of the Power Set.
The passive HP recovery stacking rule from Playing the Game applies throughout: passive per-Turn HP
recovery from multiple sources uses the highest value only and does not stack.

Astral Projection
Once per day as an Action, project your consciousness out of your body. Your projected form may travel
up to Medium range (50–120 ft) from your body. Your body remains Unconscious and is vulnerable while

Power Mimicry
Once per encounter as an Action, copy one Tier 1 Power you have directly observed being used within
